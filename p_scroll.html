Paintings Name or Title Artists: Descriptions
Spoliarium Juan Luna (1884) Juan Luna's painting Spoliarium, a large oil on canvas, depicts gladiator matches in Rome, highlighting the bloody consequences of these matches. The painting contrasts spectators eagerly waiting for fights with mourners mourning the death of a fallen gladiator. Luna's painting reflects the cruelty, injustices, sufferings, and helplessness experienced by gladiators, revealing the deprived rights of people. The largest painting in the Philippines, it won Luna his first gold medal in 1884.
Planting Rice with Mayon Volcano Fernando Amorsolo (1951) Planting Rice is a famous painting by Filipino artist Amorsolo, depicting farmers working in a field with huts in the background. It captures the traditional occupation of Filipinos and is representative of Amorsolo's childhood. The painting features backlighting, a technique that draws attention to human figures. It reflects the arrival of Americans in the Philippines and the idealism of artists in society, expressing happiness despite difficulties.
Madonna of the Slums Vicente Manansala(1950) Madonna of the Slums is a significant painting in the modernist movement in the Philippines, depicting a woman and her child moving from rural to urban areas. Manansala's work focuses on the betterment of national culture, social environment, identity, and native sensibility, and is currently on display at the National Museum Collection.
Fruit Gatherer Fernando Amorsolo(1950) The painting Fruit Gatherer by Amorsolo depicts a young girl in a rural Philippine landscape with a basket of fruits. The painting uses vibrant colors and idealizes nature and native beauty. Amorsolo's mastery of natural light is evident in the painting. In December 2009, it was auctioned off in Maryland, surpassing 19th and 20th-century European and American paintings. Fruit Gatherer is the original dalagang Filipino, referring to a device for gathering fruit from trees.
The Bird Seller Vicente Manansala(1976) The Bird Seller is a Cubist painting by Vicente Manansala, reflecting the modern life of a Filipino man. Manansala's work emphasizes native identity and sensibility, using transparent cubism technique. The painting's two-dimensional depth and juxtaposition of lines, shapes, and colors create a sense of movement and depth, reminiscent of Sunday markets and hardships.
